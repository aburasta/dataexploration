The Nazis' war on religion was never called off — it just went underground. This is how the regime went from courting Germany's churches to secretly planning to abolish Christianity altogether.

In November 1933, a Nazi church official stood before twenty thousand people in Berlin's Sportpalast and demanded that the Old Testament be thrown out and the New Testament rewritten to match National Socialism. It caused such a scandal that the regime's own hand-picked Reich Bishop had to disown him on the spot. To the public, the fringe had overreached and the Party had reined it in. That was a lie — and this episode traces how the very men who disowned Reinhold Krause on camera spent the next eight years working toward exactly what he'd said out loud.

Along the way: the empty promise of Article 24, the "German Christians" and the drive to fuse 28 fractured Protestant churches into a single Nazi-controlled Reich Church under Ludwig Müller; Hitler's private contempt for the clergy he was courting ("submissive as dogs"); the pastor Martin Niemöller and the Confessional Church that refused to submit — and the seven years Niemöller spent in Sachsenhausen and Dachau for it; Hans Kerrl declaring that "Positive Christianity" simply *was* National Socialism; Martin Bormann's flat verdict that "National Socialism and Christianity are irreconcilable"; and finally Alfred Rosenberg's thirty-point blueprint for a "National Reich Church" — a plan to end Bible printing, clear every crucifix from every altar, lay Mein Kampf on the altar with a sword beside it, and replace every cross in Germany with the swastika. It was only interrupted by military defeat.
