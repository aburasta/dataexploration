Sometime between one and three in the morning on April 29th, 1945, in a bomb-proof room forty feet under a burning Berlin, Adolf Hitler got married for the first time. Very few people in his own inner circle even knew this woman even existed. From the time they signed the documents, they had perhaps a day and a half left to live.

But for many years, Adolf Hitler told his closest followers that the great passionate love of his life was not this woman he was marrying, Eva Braun at all. It was another woman — one who had been dead since 1931. Often when people look at the lives of top Nazis, they tend to overemphasize the effects of whichever angle they are looking at them from on the evils they eventually committed. For instance, people who have looked at occultic practices of the Nazis tend to view almost every act of the holocaust as some occultic ritual. This video takes a different view. Love and passion are core human affections that can be both a symptom and underlying effect of the state of mind and personal views people tend to hold. Hitler, and the women he professed love for, especially the one he claimed to truly love can give us a window into the deepest values he held. You will come to understand the nature evil takes when it has troubled interpersonal relationships.

In his down-and-out years in Vienna, before the First World War, Hitler lived in a men's home among the shipwrecked and the drifting. The men who knew him remembered a strange, dirty stranger who had almost paralysing shyness. He was in his early twenties then. So far as anyone could establish, he had no relations of any kind with women during his entire time in the city.

The First World War didn't change him. Corporal Hitler won the Iron Cross, First Class, and wore it proudly to the end of his life. But even during that time, his comrades found him a peculiar fellow and he was known as the one man who wouldn't curse the war or the filth or the lice. No letters came for him. No parcels. He never asked for leave. And unlike the other men, he had not even a combat soldier's interest in women. Whatever was building inside Adolf Hitler, it was not ordinary human intimacy.

After Hitler was released from prison for trying to instigate a coup from Munich, he became somewhat of a mini celebrity and with the fame, eventually some relationships with women. His early biographer Konrad Heiden recounts a string of romantic partners from this period: a young woman named Jenny Haug, whose brother was Hitler's chauffeur; Erna Hanfstaengl, sister of one of his patrons who funded him in this period; even for Winifred Wagner, the daughter-in-law of the composer he worshipped, Richard Wagner. But nothing ended anywhere and Hitler had a ready reason. He was still on parole after his failed 1923 putsch and he could be sent back to prison for years at any time given he hadn't stopped the street violence he was organizing as part of his attempts to eventually seize power. "So there could be no question of marriage for me," he told his followers. "I therefore had to renounce certain opportunities that offered themselves."

In the summer of 1928, Hitler rented a villa above Berchtesgaden and brought his widowed half-sister, Angela Raubal, from Vienna to keep house. She brought her two daughters. One of them was Geli — twenty years old, with flowing blond hair. And you know Hitler and blonde hair. She was Hitler's niece. And Hitler, soon fell in love with her. He took her everywhere — to meetings, to conferences, on long walks in the mountains, to the cafés and theaters of Munich. When he rented a luxurious nine-room apartment on one of the city's most fashionable streets, Geli was given her own room in it. This was the only truly deep love affair of Adolf Hitler's life.

But then party gossip spread; some of the primmer leaders suggested Hitler either stop parading his young niece in public or marry her — and he was so enraged by the talk that he once fired a regional party boss over it. His early comrades said Geli was flattered at first and enjoyed the attention of a rising man. But he became a tyrant over her. He forbade her to be seen with any other man. He suspected she'd had an affair with his own bodyguard, an ex-convict named Emil Maurice. Because of this, he forbade her to return to Vienna to continue her pursuit of a career in singing in the opera. He wanted her, Shirer writes, for himself alone.

There is a darker layer to the love life from the account of Hitler's early biographer Heiden. Heiden tells of a letter Hitler wrote to Geli in 1929, confessing inclinations. That letter, Heiden says, fell into the hands of the son of Hitler's landlord. The compromising letter quietly bought back from the landlord's son. The money came from the party treasurer. The man who handled the retrieval was Father Bernhard Stempfle, an ex-priest and anti-Semitic journalist who had earlier helped edit the manuscript of Mein Kampf. Father Stempfle knew too much about Hitler's private grief — and, it seems, he did not keep all of it to himself. In the summer of 1934, when Hitler settled accounts with old friends now enemies by killing and forcing many of them to flee in the Night of the Long Knives, Stempfle's body was found in a forest near Munich, his neck broken and three bullets in his heart.

But years before that in September 17th, 1931, Hitler left the Munich apartment to travel to Hamburg. As he climbed into his car, neighbors heard Geli call down to him from the window: "Then you won't let me go to Vienna?" And they heard him answer: "No." The next morning, Geli Raubal was found shot dead in her room. She was twenty-three. The state's attorney, after a thorough investigation, ruled it a suicide; the coroner found the bullet was self-inflicted, through the chest. For years afterward, murky gossip circulated in Munich that she had been murdered by Hitler in a rage, or by Himmler. But no credible evidence ever surfaced to support any of it.

Whatever happened, the effect on Hitler was total. Gregor Strasser — a rival within the party — later recounted that he had to stay at Hitler's side for two days and nights to keep him from taking his own life in this time. Given special permission by the Austrian government, Hitler traveled to Geli's grave in Vienna and spent an evening weeping over it. For months he was inconsolable. Some in Hitler's own inner circle believed that it was from this blow that Hitler took his lifelong vow never to eat meat again. And to those closest to him, he declared it forever after: Geli Raubal was the only woman he ever loved. He spoke of her, they said, always with reverence, and often in tears.

Three weeks after Geli's death, Adolf Hitler had his first face-to-face meeting with President von Hindenburg for a bid for the chancellorship of Germany. It was one of the most important interviews of his life. And it went badly. Friends who knew him said he seemed not to be in full possession of his faculties, distracted, unsure of himself — and they put it down to the shock of losing Geli.

A year or two after Geli's death, in the Munich photography shop of Hitler's personal photographer Heinrich Hoffmann, a young assistant caught his eye. Her name was Eva Braun. She was the daughter of lower-middle-class Bavarian parents who at first strenuously opposed the relationship — even though the man in question was the dictator of Germany. She would be his mistress for more than twelve years. And in all that time, he kept her almost completely out of sight.

Eva Braun lived a strange life. She was acknowledged as neither wife nor mistress, barred from his headquarters, rarely permitted even to visit Berlin, left to fill her days with swimming, skiing, watching films, and grooming herself while she waited for a man who was almost never there. His own chauffeur, Erich Kempka, called her "the unhappiest woman in Germany" — a woman who, he said, spent most of her life waiting for Hitler.

She was not, it seems, the only one. Even in 1939, with Eva installed on the mountain, Mussolini's foreign minister Count Ciano recorded in his diary a rumor of the Führer's "tender feelings" for a beautiful twenty-year-old named Sigrid von Lappus — writing that they saw each other "frequently and intimately." Ciano was intrigued; he had not yet even heard of Eva Braun.

Which brings us back to April 1945. As the inner circle knew more and more that the war was lost and they could be killed or captured at any time, most of the top brass moved to save themselves but Eva Braun did the opposite. She traveled into surrounded Berlin, against his wishes, to die with him. And so, as a reward for a loyalty that had outlasted everyone else's, Hitler finally gave her the thing he had withheld for twelve years. He married her.

It was a ceremony out of a nightmare. Goebbels found a municipal councilor named Walter Wagner, fighting with a militia unit a few blocks away, and brought him underground to perform it. Barely a day later, on the afternoon of April 30th, 1945, the newlyweds retired to their rooms. A single shot was heard. Hitler had shot himself. Beside him lay Eva. She had not used her revolver — she had swallowed poison. They had been married for less than forty hours.

For a man who had spent most of his years being an outcast, the extreme dedication he felt he needed to lead his machinery of evil meant he could not have personal romance of the normal kind. He said it himself, in his own excuse for never marrying, that a wife would have interfered with his total dedication to leading his party and then his nation. And the one relationship that ever could have seriously competed with that devotion ended 1931 when his niece died.
