# ep-special — The Legal Coup (1919-1933)

(1)October 1931. Adolf Hitler sits across from the President of Germany for the first time. Paul von Hindenburg, eighty-four years old listens to the leader of the fastest-growing party in the country make his case.(1) (2)And when Hitler leaves, Hindenburg turns to the general who arranged the meeting and delivers his verdict. This "Bohemian corporal", as he calls Hitler, might make an adequate Minister of Posts one day. Chancellor of Germany? Never.(2)

(3)Fifteen months later, Paul von Hindenburg swears Adolf Hitler in as Chancellor of Germany.(3) (4)But because Hitler looks like the defining archetype of the military dictator, what many people do not realise is that Hitler did not come to power through a coup, or as they like to call it in Germany, a putsch.(4) (5)He had of course tried to use the way of the coup a decade before this meeting with Hindenburg but that failed, was imprisoned,(5) (6)had been released and he had decided to go the legal way.(6)

(7)But none of what happened in the fifteen months between the first meeting and becoming Chancellor is what anyone would expect, every month produced more dramatic turn of events than the previous one. But much of the machinations were successful because of the steps laid in the years Hitler's release from prison and even before that. Start with the first lie that made all of it possible.(7) (8)In November 1918, a British general named Neill Malcolm was having dinner with Erich Ludendorff, one of the top military leaders of Germany in the First World War and high command's real strongman, who always complained that the civilian government back home had failed the army.(8) (9)Trying to sum up the general's rambling point in a single sentence, Malcolm asked him:(9) (Q1)"Do you mean, General, that you were stabbed in the back?"(Q1) (10)Ludendorff's eyes lit up.(10)

(11)The idea of the stab in the back was fraudulent from its conception because Ludendorff himself had begged for an armistice weeks earlier, telling his own government the war was militarily lost.(11) (12)But the myth didn't need to be true. It needed only to be believable to millions of humiliated Germans looking for someone else to blame(12) (13)and Hitler, the young corporal recovering from a British gas attack in a military hospital was one of the millions who believed it instantly and completely.(13) (Q2)"The German Army had not been defeated in the field," is how Adolf Hitler liked to put it.(Q2)

(14)After his recovery, he arrived in Munich in September 1919 as a spy for the army. His job was to check out an obscure club calling itself the German Workers' Party, one of a hundred such fringe groups sprouting in the wreckage of the old empire.(14) (15)The army would send spies to these meetings. One night, he sat through a meeting of maybe twenty-five men in a beer-hall back room but the meeting was unremarkable and he had already begun leaving when a supposed professor in the audience proposed that Bavaria secede from Germany.(15) (16)Hitler stood up and tore into him so violently that the man reportedly fled the hall "like a wet poodle."(16) (17)A locksmith named Anton Drexler, the club's real founder, chased Hitler down afterward and pressed a pamphlet into his hands. Hitler was in a few short weeks enrolled as a member of what would become the Nazi party.(17)

(18)Within a year he'd taken over its propaganda wing and night after night he packed out Munich's largest beer hall for a rallies.(18) (19)By the summer of 1921, while he was traveling in Berlin trying to build outside contacts from his familiar territory of Munich and Bavaria, the committee back home decided he'd grown too dictatorial and tried to merge the party with a rival group to dilute his influence.(19) (20)Hitler raced back to Munich and offered his resignation to the party leaders, betting on his knowledge that the party couldn't survive without its only real speaker and its biggest source of donations.(20) (21)So when the leaders objected to the resignation, he now demanded absolute personal control of the party no committee.(21)

(22)Drexler and the others fought back with a pamphlet accusing Hitler of(22) (Q3)"a lust for power and personal ambition,"(Q3) (23)of using the party(23) (Q4)"as a springboard for his own immoral purposes."(Q4) (24)They weren't wrong but it didn't matter. Hitler sued them for libel and forced Drexler to publicly disavow his own pamphlet and had the party's statutes rewritten to abolish the committee altogether.(24) (25)Drexler was initially moved from his leadership details and given an honorary title and was subsequently erased from the history of the party.(25)

(26)The wider context of Germany's legal structures, like many governments of today, was completely compromised. Weimar Germany's courts were reliably lenient toward right-wing violence and merciless toward the left.(26) (27)After the murders of the Catholic politician Matthias Erzberger in 1921 and the Jewish foreign minister Walther Rathenau in 1922 by men of the extreme right for their attempts to make the peace treaty work, they got light sentences or quiet help escaping.(27) (28)But when the Spartacist rebels attempted a left wing coup which was crushed in 1919, over seven hundred people were charged with treason.(28) (29)Hitler had come to learn in his new political career that violent revolution from the right wing carried almost no legal risk.(29)

(30)In the fall of 1923, Bavaria was still administered by an unofficial triumvirate — Gustav von Kahr as state commissioner, General Otto von Lossow commanding the army in Bavaria and police chief Hans von Seisser.(30) (31)Hitler needed them or needed to force them to be on his side because he'd learned in his Vienna years that a revolution needs the cooperation of existing power and he already knew these men were defiant of Berlin and were flirting with the idea of a coup.(31) (32)When he heard Kahr had scheduled a speech at the Bürgerbräukeller beer hall on the evening of November 8th, with Lossow and Seisser both expected in the audience,(32) (33)Hitler saw his chance to trap all three in one room.(33)

(34)At a quarter to nine, with Kahr thirty minutes into his speech to about three thousand in the beer hall, Nazi storm troopers ringed the hall and Hitler pushed to the front, jumped on a table, and fired a pistol shot into the ceiling.(34) (Q5)"The National Revolution has begun!" he shouted.(Q5) (35)He claimed the barracks were occupied and the army and police already marching under the swastika. None of it was true yet. But nobody in the hall knew that.(35) (36)He marched Kahr, Lossow and Seisser at gunpoint into a side room.(36)

(37)It didn't work. All three men sat in silence, unmoved until Hitler rushed back into the hall and lied to the crowd that the triumvirate had already joined him in his stealing of power.(37) (38)All three slipped away the moment they were out of Hitler's sight, and by the time Hitler led a column of three thousand marchers into central Munich, the army and police were waiting for him.(38) (39)Shots were exchanged and sixteen Nazis and three policemen lay dead within a minute.(39) (40)Hitler dislocated his shoulder diving for the pavement in the shootout and just like that, the Beer Hall Putsch was over.(40)

(41)But that wasn't the end for National Socialism as would have been for almost any other party in this position but it was the beginning of the lesson that would put Hitler into Chancellorship less than a decade later.(41) (42)The trial for high treason against Hitler for the attempted coup opened on February 26, 1924, with a compromised judiciary and a Bavarian justice minister who was an old friend of Hitler.(42) (43)He was allowed to interrupt at will, cross-examine anyone and speak for as long as he wanted.(43) (44)The putsch and trial that should have ended him instead made him a national figure for the first time and plastered his name on front pages across Germany and the world.(44)

(45)He was eventually found guilty and sentenced to five years for an offense that, on paper, carried mandatory life imprisonment.(45) (46)He served less than nine months in total in the comfortable quarters at Landsberg prison where he dictated the first volume of what would become Mein Kampf to Rudolf Hess.(46) (47)Years later, he told his old followers that he learnt that it wasn't enough to overthrow a state by force. The new state had to be built up in advance, ready to hand, so that the old one only needed a final shove.(47)

(48)Released from Landsberg at the end of 1924, Hitler made two absolutely important decisions. First, finally concentrate every ounce of party power in his own hands and permanently. Second, pursue power exclusively through constitutional means and not use any armed force.(48) (49)He'd already explained the new strategy to a party comrade while still behind bars:(49) (Q6)"Instead of working to achieve power by armed coup, we shall have to hold our noses and enter the Reichstag against the Catholic and Marxist deputies. If outvoting them takes longer than outshooting them, at least the result will be guaranteed by their own constitution... Sooner or later we shall have a majority — and after that, Germany."(Q6)

(50)Over the next five years, he built a shadow state based on his idea that a new state to replace the old state must exist before the revolution. The country was carved into party districts mirroring the real Reichstag electoral map, each with its own appointed boss.(50) (51)They created a parallel bureaucracy with departments of agriculture, justice, economy, and race and culture within the party.(51) (52)There were youth wings, a women's league, professional guilds for doctors and lawyers and teachers, a private court system to keep the party's scandals out of public view.(52) (53)And there were two new bodies of armed men: the S.A. — brute street fighters who had been under the command of Ernst Röhm —(53) (54)and a small black-uniformed bodyguard core for Hitler called the Schutzstaffel, the SS.(54) (55)In 1929 Hitler handed the S.S. to a mild, bookish former chicken farmer Heinrich Himmler.(55)

(56)The most instructive fight of these years, though, wasn't against the Republic. It was inside the party itself.(56) (57)Gregor Strasser was a member of the party and a druggist by trade and a decorated war veteran, built an independent power base across northern Germany that Hitler did not have because his popularity was still confined mostly to Bavaria.(57) (58)Strasser had his own newspaper, his own network of district leaders, and a young recruit with a doctorate as his secretary: Joseph Goebbels.(58) (59)In November 1925, Strasser convened his own conference at Hanover for two reasons. First to push a genuinely socialist program because he was cultivating the socialism aspect of the National socialism by calling for the nationalization of big industry but secondly to informally challenge Hitler's authority.(59) (60)Goebbels at the time reportedly shouted that the party ought to expel "the petty bourgeois Adolf Hitler."(60)

(61)But Hitler did not openly fight Strasser at Hanover. He waited three months, then summoned his own conference at Bamberg — deliberately scheduled on a weekday so the northern leaders, working men with jobs, mostly couldn't attend.(61) (62)Strasser and Goebbels showed up nearly alone and badly outnumbered by Hitler's southern loyalists, were steamrolled into abandoning their program on the spot.(62) (63)Goebbels' own diary — discovered only after the war, and the only reliable window into what he actually felt that week — records as it happens in real time.(63) (Q7)"Hitler talks for two hours," he wrote the day after Bamberg. "I feel as though someone had beaten me... I no longer have complete faith in Hitler."(Q7) (64)He went to the train station afterward to console the humiliated Strasser.(64)

(65)And then Hitler moved to turn Strasser's own lieutenant into his camp. Six weeks later he summoned Goebbels back to Munich, gave him the stage at the same Bürgerbräukeller where the putsch had begun, and personally embraced him in front of the crowd after his speech.(65) (Q8)Goebbels' diary entry that night: "What a royal reception!... Hitler is always at my side."(Q8) (66)Within a month he was writing that he loved the Führer, that he bowed(66) (Q9)"to the greater man, to the political genius."(Q9) (67)By October 1926 Hitler made him Gauleiter of Berlin, and the most talented propagandist in the party belonged, from that point on, entirely to Hitler and not to Strasser.(67)

(68)But none of that internal party restructuring and bickering bought him anything at the ballot box for years.(68) (H1)In the May 1928 elections, with Germany getting more prosperous in the postwar years and the Weimar Republic looking almost finally stable, the Nazi Party polled just 810,000 votes out of thirty-one million cast which converted to about a dozen seats out of nearly five hundred.(H1) (69)Hitler was mostly the butt of jokes told in connection with the failed beer-hall stunt five years earlier.(69) (70)Then, in October 1929, the American stock market collapsed, and within two years German industrial production had been cut nearly in half and unemployment climbed past six million.(70) (71)Hitler did not pretend to understand economics — by his own later admission, he barely tried to. But he understood exactly what to do with the mass desperation at hand.(71) (Q10)"Never in my life have I been so well disposed and inwardly contented as in these days," he wrote in the Nazi press.(Q10)

(72)There was another audience Hitler was courting at the same time who were as important as the voters: the men with money.(72) (73)Fritz Thyssen, the steel magnate, had been giving the party money since 1923 and would later write a book confessing it, bitterly, called *I Paid Hitler*.(73) (74)Emil Kirdorf, the coal baron who ran a political slush fund known as the Ruhr Treasury, was also brought in as a financier at party congress in 1929.(74) (75)By 1931 the former Reichsbank president Hjalmar Schacht had met Hitler personally and begun devoting himself to introducing the Nazi leader to his banking and industrial friends, eventually writing him a letter that promised:(75) (Q11)"No matter where my work may take me in the near future, even if someday you should see me imprisoned in a fortress, you can always count on me as your loyal supporter".(Q11) (76)Not every industrialist became part of the club but enough of them did that by 1932, the party's fleet of chartered aircraft and its enormous private armies were being subsidized by the kind of "money barons" its own street-level rhetoric spent every day denouncing.(76)

(77)But the factor that most helped Hitler's assumption of the chancellorship was the Weimar Constitution itself. It let the President rule by decree in a national emergency, bypassing the Reichstag entirely.(77) (78)In practice, once Chancellor Heinrich Brüning started leaning on it in 1930 to force through budget measures that the Reichstag wouldn't pass, it became the norm for every government after him.(78) (79)Parliament responded by voting down Brüning's decrees;(79) (80)Brüning got President Hindenburg to dissolve the Reichstag and call new elections instead of ever actually winning a majority on the issue.(80)

(H2)The September 1930 election that followed was the moment the Nazi Party stopped being a fringe. Hitler had hoped to quadruple his 1928 vote and pick up maybe fifty seats. He got over six million votes and 107 seats — a nine-fold jump overnight and suddenly, the party went from smallest in the Reichstag to second-largest.(H2) (81)This shook up the army's officer corps and Germany's industrialists.(81) (82)Hitler had already been courting the generals directly, telling an audience in 1929 that the army's future lay with parties who'd defend its interests, not with a democracy that(82) (Q12)"stipulates the dissolution of all that which is the basis of the existence of an army."(Q12) (83)When three young Reichswehr lieutenants were put on trial for treason in 1930 after trying to recruit fellow officers to a pro-Nazi mutiny, Hitler testified for the defense and used the witness stand, as he had used during his own trial for the coup, to promise the officer corps he had no intention of replacing the army, only of expanding it.(83) (84)Asked by the presiding judge whether his revolution would come "by constitutional means," Hitler answered, simply,(84) (Q13)"Yes."(Q13) (85)On that particular promise to expand the army despite the restrictions imposed by the Treaty of Versailles, industrialists like Fritz Thyssen and bankers like Hjalmar Schacht were already betting he might be telling the truth and their money started flowing accordingly.(85)

(86)That first, humiliating meeting with Hindenburg in October 1931 was followed by a second lesson in how carefully Hitler had to manage his own allies.(86) (87)He travelled straight from the Chancellery to join the old-guard conservative opposition of Alfred Hugenberg's Nationalists, the veterans' militia known as the Stahlhelm, and a group of retired imperial generals at a mass demonstration meant to present a unified "National Opposition" against both the government and Prussia's Social Democrats.(87) (88)Hitler showed up, spoke, and left early, before the Stahlhelm's own parade even finished, visibly contemptuous of what he privately regarded as relics on a past time who still thought history belonged to them.(88) (89)He needed their money and their respectability for the moment. He had absolutely no intention of taking orders from them, or of letting his movement be swallowed into their coalition.(89) (90)Within days of this demonstration, the so-called Harzburg Front had collapsed into the same infighting it was supposed to overcome.(90) (91)Hitler's strategy of alliance-making in this period made him the ultimate victor of this collapse because he used every faction that can hand him money or legitimacy but never actually merged with any of them.(91)

(92)By February 1932, Hitler faced a problem that could not be overcome by just political strategy however. He wasn't a German citizen, and Germany's constitution didn't let foreigners run for President.(92) (93)The party's fix for this was: on February 25th, the Nazi interior minister of the tiny state of Brunswick appointed Hitler an attaché of Brunswick's legation in Berlin.(93) (94)This was a nothing job of course but it automatically, under German law, made him a citizen of Brunswick and therefore of the Reich.(94)

(95)The new election race in 1932 was Hitler against the eighty-four-year-old Hindenburg and that produced Hitler's first real national campaign — chartering an airplane to hit three or four cities a day, a tactic unprecedented in German electioneering and plastering the country with a million posters and using sound trucks and gramophone records to reach crowds.(95) (H3)But he still lost both rounds. Hindenburg took the runoff with fifty-three percent to Hitler's thirty-seven.(H3) (96)It was, on its face, a decisive rejection of the extremes by the German electorate — and it should have been the end of the story.(96) (97)Instead, a few days before Hindenburg's re-election was even finalized, the government tried to strike down the Nazis permanently by Hindenburg signing a decree on April 13th, 1932 banning Hitler's street fighting gang, the S.A. outright,(97) (98)after police in Berlin seized documents suggesting these brownshirts already outnumbered the actual German army and were preparing a coup.(98)

(99)The ban should have crippled the party. It didn't last ninety days however because General Kurt von Schleicher, the army's most important political operator, who'd engineered Brüning's own rise to the chancellorship in the first place and now decided Brüning and his own patron, Defense Minister Wilhelm Groener, had both outlived their usefulness.(99) (100)Schleicher worked behind the scenes to undermine the S.A. ban he'd initially approved and spread a smear campaign against Groener suggesting he was senile and pro-Marxist, and also reopened secret contact with both Röhm and Hitler.(100) (101)Within a month Groener was humiliated on the Reichstag floor and forced to resign;(101) (102)three weeks after that, Hindenburg dismissed Brüning too and on Schleicher's engineering, replaced him with a compliant aristocrat named Franz von Papen.(102) (103)Papen who now owed his job entirely to Schleicher's first act in office was to lift the ban on the S.A. entirely.(103) (104)Political killings in the streets of Prussia spiked immediately — eighty-two dead in three weeks in June alone.(104)

(105)On July 20th, 1932, using the excuse of a riot in a Hamburg suburb, Papen invoked Article 48 to depose the elected Social Democratic government of Prussia — by far the largest German state, and the last one still run by people genuinely interested in democracy — and appointed himself Reich Commissioner in its place.(105) (106)No mass uprising followed. The Prussian Social Democrats had their own police force and could plausibly have resisted but chose not to risk a general strike.(106) (107)Hitler could of course tell by this that there was no longer any organized force in Germany prepared to physically defend democracy.(107)

(H4)The July 1932 election that followed handed the Nazis their all-time electoral peak: 37 percent of the vote, 230 seats, comfortably the largest party in the Reichstag but still nowhere near a majority.(H4) (109)Hitler met with Schleicher days later and demanded the full chancellorship plus a slate of key ministries along with an enabling act to let him rule by decree. He had assumed Schleicher was on his side and agreed because of his favourable stance towards the Nazis. But Schleicher turned him down.(109) (H5)Three months later, in the November 1932, another election happened and the Nazi vote actually fell. They lost two million votes and thirty-four seats.(H5) (111)The industrialists panicked and started withdrawing the financing of the Nazis. With money drying up, even Goebbels was writing in his diary about the party's chronic debt situation.(111) (112)And while we refrain from giving too much political commentary on this channel, it is worth pointing out that Hitler became chancellor about 10 weeks from this point. He was far from unstoppable popular momentum. He was losing but what happens next that brings him to power is a lesson on how democracies should not sleepwalk into fascism.(112)

(113)With Papen's government collapsing under a no-confidence vote and the Nazis visibly declining, Schleicher made his own bid, convincing Hindenburg he could split the Nazi movement by peeling off Gregor Strasser — still the second most powerful man in the party — with an offer of the vice-chancellorship. And it came close to working.(113) (114)Strasser was firstly opposed to Hitler because he was one of the last leading remnants of the Socialism ideals the party was founded on and Hitler no longer pretended to choose a certain specific side of the economic model debate. He would say and align with whoever and whatever principle to appeal to the masses.(114) (115)Strasser was also convinced Hitler's all-or-nothing strategy had run the party into a dead end, confronted Hitler directly on December 7th in a shouting match at the Kaiserhof hotel.(115) (116)He went back to his room and wrote out his resignation from every post he held in the party.(116)

(117)Goebbels' diary records the panic that followed.(117) (Q14)"The letter landed like a bombshell."(Q14) (118)Hitler paced his hotel room for hours, then stopped and said that if the party fell apart, he would end it all with a pistol shot in three minutes.(118) (119)But that didn't happen because Strasser simply gave up and left for a vacation in Italy instead of following up with organizing the party split.(119) (120)Hitler seized the party machinery as soon as Strasser left and purged the allies of Strasser from the party and had every remaining party leader sign a fresh personal loyalty oath.(120)

(121)Schleicher took the chancellorship himself on December 2nd, the first general to hold the post since Otto von Bismarck's era and his chancellorship lasted fifty-seven days.(121) (122)In his own later words:(122) (Q15)"I stayed in power only fifty-seven days, and on each and every one of them I was betrayed fifty-seven times."(Q15) (123)Franz von Papen, thirsting for revenge because Schleicher had undermined him to displace him from the chancellorship, reached out on December 10th to a Cologne banker named Baron Kurt von Schröder who was a financial contact for the Nazis to arrange a secret sit-down with Hitler.(123)

(124)In that meeting in Schröder's home on the morning of January 4th, 1933, Papen and Hitler — who had been bitter rivals only months earlier — spent two hours agreeing in principle to jointly replace Schleicher's government with Hitler as Chancellor and Papen's allies filling out the cabinet under him.(124)

(125)The final consolidation happened on the night of January 22nd, in the suburban Berlin home of a wine merchant man named Joachim von Ribbentrop.(125) (126)President Hindenburg's own son, Colonel Oskar von Hindenburg, and his father's state secretary, Otto Meissner took a taxi to Ribbentrop's house to meet Hitler, Papen, Göring, and Frick.(126) (127)Hitler asked to speak to Oskar von Hindenburg alone and the two men withdrew to another room for a full hour.(127) (128)Meissner later testified under oath at Nuremberg that on the taxi ride home, Oskar von Hindenburg said there was no option but to bring the Nazis into government.(128) (129)A few months later, five thousand tax-free acres were quietly added to the Hindenburg family's East Prussian estate. In August 1934, Oskar jumped two full ranks in the army.(129) (130)Schleicher, feeling cornered in the January of 1933 after the news of all these secret meetings went around, went to Hindenburg on January 23rd and asked for dictatorial emergency powers to suspend the Reichstag altogether and rule as a military government. Hindenburg refused and sent him away to keep looking for a parliamentary majority.(130) (131)Schleicher resigned on January 28th.(131)

(132)Two days of frantic rumors followed and at one point Hitler's own people believed that Schleicher and the army were about to stage a military coup and cart Hindenburg off to his country estate, and Hitler mobilized the Berlin S.A. and a friendly police force on standby just in case.(132) (133)On the morning of January 30th, 1933, the men who would form Hitler's cabinet gathered at Papen's house.(133) (134)Papen was confident by his own account, that he had built a government he could fully control. He had agreed that Hitler would be chancellor and he would be the only one allowed to see the Chancellor in private while having most of the cabinet under his control: eight of his own surrounding just three Nazis.(134)

(135)At half past eleven, Hindenburg delivered a short speech about the necessity of national unity, and this cabinet was sworn in.(135) (136)And with an anticlimactic end to a dramatic politicking process, the former tramp from Vienna was now Chancellor of Germany because Papen had convinced himself he could control Hitler and the Nazis and their partnership in the Reichstag would create the needed majority to bring Germany under his control.(136)

(137)Papen's confidence lasted twenty-eight days. On the night of February 27th, 1933, the Reichstag building caught fire. Hitler and Goebbels were at Goebbels' home when the call came.(137) (138)By the time they raced to the scene, Göring was already there, and already shouting to the new Gestapo chief that this was the opening act of a Communist uprising and that every Communist official had to be shot on sight.(138) (139)The full truth of the fire per the evidence assembled at Nuremberg points overwhelmingly toward Nazi planning.(139) (Q16)Years later, at a private lunch on Hitler's birthday, Göring reportedly slapped his own thigh and told the table: "The only one who really knows about the Reichstag is I, because I set it on fire!"(Q16)

(140)Within twenty-four hours, Hitler had Hindenburg's signature on a decree suspending freedom of the press, of assembly, of private correspondence, authorizing search and seizure without warrant, and imposing the death penalty for a list of loosely defined offenses.(140) (141)Four thousand Communist officials and many Social Democrats were rounded up within days,(141) (142)but the reality was the manner the Nazis behave in the wake of this decree became the permanent legal architecture of the entire Nazi police state and it stayed in force until 1945.(142)

(143)Another election followed a week later on March 5th but this time, the Nazis had the full machinery of the state so opposition newspapers were banned, opponents' candidates arrested(143) (H6)and with that, Hitler's party won only 44 percent of the vote.(H6) (145)A clear majority of Germans, even under those conditions, still did not vote Nazi and they were nowhere close to the two-thirds needed to change the constitution itself.(145)

(146)So on March 21st, in the Garrison Church at Potsdam Hitler opened the new Reichstag in a ceremony built to impress old Hindenburg. Hindenburg delivered a short blessing over the new government.(146) (147)Two days later, the Enabling Act, formally titled the "Law for Removing the Distress of People and Reich," was passed including the power to write laws that openly contradicted the constitution itself.(147) (148)To pass it required a two-thirds majority Hitler didn't have so the eighty-one Communist deputies were simply barred from the chamber, a number of Social Democrats were already under arrest,(148) (149)and storm troopers lined the opera house aisles in an unsubtle threat to anyone still tempted to vote no.(149)

(H7)The final vote was 441 to 84. Every no came from the Social Democrats — the only party in the entire Reichstag that voted against its own extinction to the end.(H7) (151)On March 9th, before the vote was even two weeks old, storm troopers simply walked into the Bavarian state government and replaced it with Nazi officials.(151) (152)Every other German state followed within a month, their governors now hand-picked appointees answerable only to Berlin and for the first time in German history the government had actually erased the country's federal structure.(152) (153)And despite his promise to protect trade unions when he was pretending to be a socialist for votes, trade unions were seized in a single coordinated action that May.(153) (154)By July, every remaining political party in Germany was banned outright with some parties like the Catholic parties dissolving themselves rather than wait to be crushed.(154) (155)A law that July made it official and permanent: the Nazi Party was now, by statute, the only legal party in Germany.(155)

(156)In the end, the intricate system put in place to ensure there was no domination of one single party led to numerous failed coalitions and a series of elections and switching alliances, bad planning and disastrous greed on all involved ended up creating an absolutist regime that would torment the rest of Europe for the next 10 years. But that is a story for another series.(156)
