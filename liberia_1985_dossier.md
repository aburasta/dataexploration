# THE COUP THAT FAILED — LIBERIA, 1985
## A Documentary Research Dossier

*Thomas Quiwonkpa's 12 November 1985 attempt to overthrow Samuel Doe — the coup that held Monrovia for a morning, then unleashed a catastrophe.*

## 1. THE STORY IN 90 SECONDS

For one morning in November 1985, it looked like the good guys had won.

A month earlier, Liberia's military ruler **Samuel Doe** had stolen a national election in plain sight. Then, on **12 November 1985**, a familiar voice came over the national radio: **Thomas Quiwonkpa** — the popular general who had helped bring Doe to power in 1980, before Doe turned on him and drove him into exile — announced that he was back, and that Doe was finished. Monrovia poured into the streets to celebrate. People danced. For a few hours, the dictatorship seemed over. **[FACT]**

It wasn't. Quiwonkpa had crossed in from Sierra Leone with only a couple dozen men. He announced victory before he had confirmed Doe was dead or captured — and Doe wasn't. By mid-afternoon Doe was back on the radio; his Krahn-dominated soldiers regrouped, and within a day the coup collapsed. Quiwonkpa was hunted down, killed, his body mutilated and displayed — and, by many accounts, partly eaten by soldiers who believed it would transfer his power. **[FACT; cannibalism ALLEGED]**

Then came the real horror. Doe's forces took their revenge on Quiwonkpa's own people — the **Gio and Mano** of **Nimba County** — in a wave of ethnic killing. The survivors would become the recruits for **Charles Taylor's** 1989 invasion. The failed coup of 1985 is widely seen as the spark that lit a **14-year civil war** that killed more than a quarter of a million people. **[FACT]**

## 2. CHARACTER DOSSIER

### THOMAS QUIWONKPA — the coup leader
- **The making of him:** Born 1955 in Zurlay, Nimba County, to subsistence farmers; an ethnic **Gio (Dan)**. Joined the army at 16. Rose to national fame as a co-architect of the **12 April 1980 coup** that ended 133 years of Americo-Liberian rule. Doe made him a general and **commanding general of the army** in his mid-twenties. He was widely seen as **more popular and more principled than Doe**, especially among indigenous Liberians — the revolution's idealist. **[FACT]**
- **The rupture:** As Doe purged rivals and tilted power toward his own Krahn group, Quiwonkpa was **demoted in 1983**, accused of plotting, and fled into exile — via Côte d'Ivoire, then the United States (reportedly Baltimore). **[FACT]**
- **Role:** Organizer and figurehead of the 1985 attempt.
- **Fate:** Captured and killed **15 November 1985** by Krahn soldiers; body mutilated and publicly displayed. **[FACT]** Reports that soldiers castrated the corpse and **ate parts of it** for ritual "power" are **widely and consistently reported but not forensically proven** — present as a reported atrocity. **[ALLEGED]**

### SAMUEL KANYON DOE — the target
- **The making of him:** Born 1951, ethnic **Krahn** from impoverished Grand Gedeh County; a **master sergeant** with little formal schooling. Led the **1980 coup** — killing President Tolbert and, days later, **publicly executing 13 officials on a Monrovia beach**. Became the first indigenous (non-Americo-Liberian) ruler of Liberia. **[FACT]**
- **Role:** The target. By 1985, increasingly autocratic, reliant on Krahn loyalists, and propped up by US Cold-War money.
- **Fate:** Captured, tortured and killed in **1990** by Prince Johnson's faction — the torture infamously **videotaped**. The grotesque symmetry with Quiwonkpa's death closed a circle of revenge. **[FACT]**

### JACKSON DOE — the man who probably won (no relation)
- A respected former Nimba senator; ran for president in 1985 for the Liberian Action Party. **[FACT]** Officially placed second; **the BBC and many independent observers believed he actually won an outright majority** before Doe's hand-picked committee counted ballots in secret. **[FACT/widely held]** The central symbol of the stolen election. Killed during the war in 1990 — **manner and who ordered it DISPUTED.**

### CHARLES TAYLOR — the shadow over the aftermath
- **[FACT]** Served in Doe's government, fled embezzlement charges, and **escaped a Massachusetts jail on 15 September 1985** — days before Quiwonkpa's coup. Described as Quiwonkpa's "friend." He later built the **NPFL**, invading Nimba in December 1989 and recruiting heavily among the Gio/Mano whom Doe's reprisals had brutalized.
- **[ALLEGED]** Taylor's own later courtroom claims that the **CIA armed Quiwonkpa** and helped him escape jail are **uncorroborated, self-serving, and denied** — there is no firm evidence he was operationally part of the 1985 coup itself.

### PRINCE YORMIE JOHNSON — the protégé who closed the circle
- Born 1952, Nimba County, ethnic Gio. **Quiwonkpa's aide-de-camp**, who went into exile with him in 1983. **[FACT]** Later joined Taylor's NPFL, then broke away; on **9 September 1990 his faction captured and killed Doe** — read across Nimba as revenge for Quiwonkpa. Became a senator; died **28 November 2024.** **[FACT]**

### THOMAS WEH SYEN & the PRC (context)
- A leading figure of the 1980 coup and Doe's deputy. **Executed by firing squad with four others on 14 August 1981** on an alleged plot, no public evidence shown. **[FACT]** Established Doe's pattern of **liquidating his own comrades** — the same pattern that drove Quiwonkpa out.

### THE ETHNIC FAULT LINE — Krahn vs. Gio/Mano
- The 1980 coup was multi-ethnic, but Doe came to rule through his own **Krahn** group (~5% of the population), stacking the army's **Executive Mansion Guard** with Krahn loyalists and marginalizing the **Gio and Mano** of Nimba. **[FACT]** This is the fault line the failed coup detonated — and the engine of everything that followed.

## 3. FOUNDING HISTORY — WHY THE GROUND WAS LOADED

- **A republic founded by settlers.** From **1822**, the American Colonization Society resettled **freed and freeborn African Americans** on the West African coast; the capital **Monrovia** was named for US President James Monroe. Liberia declared **independence in 1847** — Africa's first republic. From the start it carried a **settler-vs-indigenous** fault line and a paternalistic bond with the United States. **[FACT]**
- **133 years of one-party settler rule.** The **Americo-Liberian** elite (a few percent of the population) monopolized power through the **True Whig Party** from **1878 to 1980**. Indigenous Liberians couldn't vote until **1951**. **[FACT]**
- **The cracks:** The **1979 "rice riots"** — sparked by a proposed hike in the price of the staple — saw security forces fire on crowds (**[DISPUTED]** toll, commonly **~40–50+ killed**), shattering the old regime's legitimacy. **[FACT]**
- **The 1980 coup:** Master Sergeant **Doe** and a group of NCOs (including **Quiwonkpa**) killed Tolbert and staged the **beach executions** of 13 officials. The **People's Redemption Council** took power. **[FACT]**
- **Doe consolidates:** purges (Weh Syen executed 1981; Quiwonkpa pushed out 1983) and growing Krahn favoritism. **[FACT]**
- **The trigger — the stolen election of 15 October 1985:** Doe credited with **50.9%** (just over the runoff threshold); ballots counted in secret by a hand-picked committee; **both houses of the US Congress declared it fraudulent.** The coup came one month later. **[FACT]**

## 4. THE STAKES — RESOURCES, MONEY, AND THE COLD WAR

- **An extractive, foreign-owned economy.** **Iron ore** was the spine — **over half of export earnings** by the 1970s; Liberia was a **leading global exporter** ([DISPUTED] 3rd vs 5th), centered on the **LAMCO** concession in the Nimba range. **[FACT]**
- **The Firestone rubber empire.** A **1926 concession** leased up to **1 million acres** at six cents an acre; **Harbel** became the world's largest contiguous rubber plantation and, for a time, **more than half of Liberia's tax revenue** — a century-long American corporate presence. **[FACT]**
- **The flag-of-convenience registry.** Liberia ran one of the world's largest ship registries (≈a quarter of world tonnage by 1978), a steady **US-administered hard-currency stream** (≈5–12% of revenue). **[FACT; a "70%" figure that surfaced is unreliable — DISPUTED]**
- **A bankrupting state.** By the mid-1980s: external debt past **100% of national income**, IMF arrears from **1984**, and tens of millions skimmed yearly from a ~$200M budget. **[FACT]**
- **Why Doe survived — the US.** Liberia was Washington's **West African Cold-War hub**: **two Voice of America transmitters**, an **OMEGA navigation station**, the **main CIA station in Africa**, and **Roberts International Airport**. Doe got roughly **$402M in US aid (1981–85)** — the **largest per-capita US aid recipient in sub-Saharan Africa** — and two Reagan meetings. **[FACT]** This is the answer to *why the coup, even when it briefly succeeded, could never be allowed to stick*: Washington had bet its regional infrastructure on Doe and **defended even his stolen election.** **[FACT]**
- **What capturing Monrovia meant:** control of the **US aid spigot**, concession rents, the registry cash, and the Krahn-dominated security forces — the whole patronage machine.

## 5. THE PLOT — HOW IT WAS MEANT TO WORK

- **The opening:** the stolen October election destroyed any peaceful path to removing Doe and set the timing. **[FACT]**
- **The infiltration:** Quiwonkpa **crossed in from Sierra Leone** with a small force — secondary accounts say **"about two dozen" heavily armed men** (army deserters/rebels). **[FACT; force size DISPUTED]**
- **[ALLEGED]** Doe claimed **"400 'devil forces'"** plus Ghanaian/Guinean/Sierra Leonean mercenaries and even Cuban troops, and blamed the **Sierra Leone** government (Momoh/ISU) for sponsoring it. Sierra Leone **denied** it; the inflated numbers are propaganda-tinged. The neutral fact: the fighters **transited Sierra Leonean territory.**
- **The morning of 12 November:** seized the national radio (**ELBC**) and the main barracks (**Barclay Training Center**); arrested several of Doe's ministers; **Quiwonkpa announced on radio that he had taken power and Doe was in hiding.** **[FACT]**
- **The apparent victory:** for several hours his forces **controlled the capital**, and **huge crowds celebrated** in the streets. The coup genuinely *appeared to have won.* **[FACT]**

## 6. HOW IT WENT WRONG — THE FATAL HOURS

The reversal came from a handful of compounding errors — the tragedy is how avoidable they look:

1. **He announced victory before confirming Doe was dead or captured.** Doe was neither. That single gap let Doe re-emerge on state radio by mid-afternoon to declare he was still in charge — which rallied the loyalists. **[FACT]**
2. **Doe's Krahn-dominated units regrouped.** The Executive Mansion Guard and garrison troops (reportedly trained by Israeli instructors) counterattacked and broke the coup within roughly a day. **[FACT]**
3. **Quiwonkpa lost contact with his own forces.** After his men herded the captured ministers into a stockade, he lost communication with his military support; loyalists broke in, freed the ministers, and Quiwonkpa fled. **[FACT]**
4. **The force was simply too small** once the loyalists rallied — two dozen men cannot hold a capital against a regrouped army. Secondary accounts fault his "unorthodox methods" and the absence of outside (US) backing. **[FACT/analysis]**
- **The end:** Quiwonkpa was cornered near **Elwa Junction** outside Monrovia and **killed on 15 November**; his body was displayed and mutilated. **[FACT]** The cannibalism of his remains is **widely reported but unproven.** **[ALLEGED]**

## 7. THE AFTERMATH — FROM ONE MORNING TO FOURTEEN YEARS

- **The Nimba reprisals.** Doe's Krahn-dominated forces, led by **General Charles Julu** of the Executive Mansion Guard, launched ethnically-targeted killings of **Gio and Mano** civilians in **Nimba County** — mostly people *not* involved in the coup. The TRC documented round-ups at the LAMCO compound and mass killings, with bodies thrown down a disused **mine shaft**. **[FACT]**
  - **[DISPUTED] Death toll:** estimates range from **"hundreds"** (Amnesty/HRW, contemporaneous) to **~3,000** (later summaries). No precise, independently verified count exists — present as a range.
- **The Monrovia crackdown:** opposition leaders (including **Jackson Doe**, Kpolleh, Kesselly, and **Ellen Johnson Sirleaf** — adopted by Amnesty as a prisoner of conscience) arrested; journalists jailed; newspapers shut and burned. **[FACT]**
- **The road to war:** the reprisals **hardened the Krahn–Gio/Mano hatred**. When **Charles Taylor's NPFL invaded Nimba on 24 December 1989**, thousands of brutalized Gio and Mano joined him for revenge. **[FACT]**
- **The US:** kept backing Doe despite the fraud and the killings, for Cold-War reasons — pulling back only late in the decade. **[FACT]**
- **The reckoning:** Doe captured, tortured to death (on video) by **Prince Johnson** in **1990**. The two civil wars (1989–2003) killed **more than 250,000** people. The **Liberian TRC** later tied the 1985 reprisals directly to the catastrophe and recommended prosecutions — largely unimplemented. **[FACT]**

## 9. DOCUMENTARY ANGLES — NARRATIVE THREADS

1. **The morning it almost worked:** the few hours of jubilation — the most cinematic, most heartbreaking in-point.
2. **Two comrades, two coups:** Quiwonkpa and Doe rose together in 1980 and destroyed each other — a betrayal story.
3. **The avoidable blunder:** announcing victory before confirming the enemy was beaten — a tragedy of a single missing step.
4. **Mirror deaths:** Quiwonkpa's mutilation in 1985 and Doe's filmed torture in 1990 — the circle of revenge.
5. **The spark:** how one failed coup and its ethnic reprisals seeded a 14-year war — the small event with the enormous shadow.
6. **Small America:** the US installations and aid that kept Doe standing even when his own people had turned.
